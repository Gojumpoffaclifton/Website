salsista & dancing app

search, dance, track

A one week sprint to design a salsa dancing app for a classmate who has a passion for dance, fitness and travel.

Skills used

  • Competitive/ comparative analysis
  • User interviews
  • Affinity mapping
  • User case
  • User flows
  • Concept mapping
  • Task Analysis
  • Wireframes
  • Rapid Prototyping

Tools used

  • Omnigraffle
  • Sketch3
  • inVision
  • Microsoft Office

My Role

I was challenged to design an app in a one week sprint. I designed this app specifically for a classmate who has passion in travel, and salasa dancing. She had moved to Seattle and was having challenges finding like-minded people her age who had a salsa dancing passion as great as hers. I used my design process and business background as sole UX designer for this prototype.

The Opportunity and Solution

Challenge

Moving to a new city can be complicated, but finding like-minded people who enjoy dancing as much as you do could potentially help. Dancing also is changing, instead of building a prototype for just finding places to dance. I was challenged to design for fitness and dance combined.

Solution

Salsista dancing app streamlines the search, planning process while adding a fitness feature. These features help users enjoy searching for a place to meet up, avoid unnecessary stress while allowing them to focus on enjoying their dancing experience.

Research and Planning

Research

I completed comparative/ competitive analysis on a number of apps that provided users the ability to search and look at places to dance. However I also documented organizational research about Trip Advisor, Instagram, and Yelp. Once understanding the user and customer experience I started a Heuristic analysis of Yelps existing mobile app. Once I completed this research I wanted to gather more data and insight from user interviews.

Planning

I completed using user flows of how this app would bridge the gap from the data I collected while also including business strategy. I created scenarios, affinity diagrams and design requirements. Once completing the affinity diagram I wanted to interview two more users to make sure my data was on the track of my design requirements.

Goals and Design

Goals

  • Streamline the usability: a easy-to-use interface with options to view information, photographs, locations, and track calories burnt while dancing if desired. Keep the menu out of the way to get information fast, quickly and solve the users questions/problem.
  • Connectivity: the interface to be designed where users can see what others are up to, and communicate effectively over various topics/ subjects.
  • Customization: the global navigation to provide users with simple screens, but able to move menu items in replace of a built in option. Giving the user full control of their experience.

Design

Once I had completed the planning stage, I wanted to make sure my design would meet the requirements based of the user data. I first started to sketch with pen and paper some rough wireframes to start my design concept mapping. Once I completed the rough wireframes I drew out some user flows on a white board to make sure the design of the app would match up with the user experience. Once they matched up I made another set of wireframes.

User Testing and Reflection

User Testing

Once the gray scale prototype was created in Sketch, I wanted to get a better feel on how the user experience would be from hands on perspective. I uploaded my design into Invision and allowed user testing to be done on my own phone. I took notes with pen and paper while listening and observing users go through the prototype.

Reflection

  • I was surprised how many users were really interested in being able to search, experience and track data while on an app specifically for dancing. I obtained a lot of positive critiques on the UI and experience. I also obtained some feedback that due to the gray scale style it was hard to fully understand some of the information architecture and interface.
  • Usability testing provided me with the data that there needed to be more data/education given to the user, to almost help them along while going through the app.
  • Finally I wish I would have done more user testing on paper prototypes or quick wireframes to fully discover where users desire information/education and where they would like to obtain as much information as possible quickly and efficiently.

pivit

investing, with capital one

Capital One's Pivit app A three week sprint in which my team and I researched, planned and created a mobile app prototype designed to appeal to millennials who are interested in investing their money, but are not sure how to begin.

Skills used

  • Competitive/ comparative analysis
  • User interviews
  • Affinity mapping
  • User case
  • User flows
  • Concept map

Tools used

Omnigraffle, Illustrator, Sketch3, inVision, Excel

My Role

I was a part of a 3 person team, in which I was the lead visual designer. I assisted in the research phase, by conducting interviews, recording and compiling data. For the planning phase, I partook in the analyzing, assessing and compiling both the quantitative and qualitative data and mapping it out according to matching affinities. I created the first user flow and for the final phase of testing, we all took turns moderating the test and recording the data.

The Opportunity Capital One wants to become known more as a tech forward company and less as solely a bank or a credit card company. One way they plan to do this is to reach out to millennials who are notoriously untrusting of the Wall Street financial companies, and get them to start investing their money. Millennials, was our target group which we define as people between the ages of 20-34 years old. Contrary to popular belief, our target group does have money, and are willing to put money into savings. 52 percent of Facebook users who fit into our demographic are considered affluent. In our research, we found that this group did not really think about investing until they have a major life event (e.g. buying a house). Their number one financial priority was paying off debt followed by saving for a house and retirement. The approach To get millennial's to start investing their money, we felt it necessary to design an app that builds up trust between the user and Capital One. Our research showed that trust was very important, especially when it comes to linking up bank accounts to their phones. They have shown that they are willing to do this, as most have apps to their banks, and also most had Venmo, which is a person to person transfer of money app. In our user interviews, we dug deeper to find out how this trust was being built. First it was familiarity (word of mouth from friends), then it was the 2 step verification process within the app and heightening security. The Steps To gain trust we did four things. First we established a progressive disclosure process that leads the user through a course of data entry built with sliders. Here, the user can freely slide inputs and enter and adjust financial figures in the calculator, without having to create an account. Our research showed that part of building trust was demonstrating the usefulness of the app for free. Second we wanted to create a 2-step verification process. This was an important step established in our interviews and an easy way to build trust. Third, I devised a concept for a progress bar that was based on the user’s goals. Once the account-making process has been started, an outline of the user’s goal would start to form. In the prototype, this is static, building as each screen progresses in the flow. In reality, it would animate, progressing through the flow. In the prototype example, a house is being built as you progress through the on-board process. The user’s investment goal was established in the first section of the onboarding. The user can establish a goal for saving up for a house, a gift for somebody, a new car, or just financial security. In our research, we found that millennials don’t really start thinking about investing until they had a major life event. This became the catalyst for them to make a pivot in their life. Lastly, we wanted to have an option to explore investing on your own, or use a proven portfolio balancing method that we can show has historically yielded higher ROI than personal investing strategies. Our data suggest that millennials trust an algorithm more than a personal financial investor. Once the whole process is completed, the app brings you to a dashboard, where you can see a graph showing how your investment is growing, and how you’re progressing towards your personal goals. Each time you open the app, the house continues to be constructed, based on how close you are to achieving your goal. The Struggles n any financial app, there are multiple layers of legal and financial disclosures that have to be gone through. This means a lot of very sensitive personal information has to be passed back and forth. Even though millennials seem to trust sharing personal information more easily, there are still inherent trust issues with this process. Another struggle in trying to create an app in three-week sprint, is time. When teaming up with a new group of people, it takes trial & error to establish the best method to start working with all cylinders. When there is research, planning, designing, and testing to do, and everyone wants their hands on every part, it often gets a little disjointed. It is my belief that we needed another week to fully test this app and make the necessary iterations to get it fully where I feel, conceptually, it could be. Next steps It seems that there is interest in this product, but it needs to be iterated on before sending it out for more thorough testings. The next steps are the following: fixing the copy to have a consistent tone, and use a more common language Iterating on the dashboard to make it high-fidelity making sure the content is understandable adding a round up option for more investing opportunities integrating a stock trading option and testing to see if it is needed/wanted making sure the automatic ETF investing information is accurate and easy to understand across generations
